Motion Graphs Practice Problems (With Answers)

Free worksheet (12 problems with answers) — ready to use for Grades 6–8

Motion graphs are one of the most powerful tools in middle school physics — and one of the most commonly misread. A distance–time (d–t) graph shows how far an object has travelled over time: the steeper the slope, the faster the object is moving. A horizontal line means the object is stationary. A velocity–time (v–t) graph shows how an object's speed changes over time: a straight upward slope means constant acceleration, a downward slope means deceleration, and a flat line means constant velocity. The area under a velocity–time graph equals the distance travelled — a key concept that students need to practise before it sticks.

These motion graph practice problems are built for middle school teachers who need reliable, graph-interpretation questions without spending prep time. All graphs are described in text with data tables so you can reproduce them on the board or in a worksheet in seconds. Use one as a bell ringer, assign a set for homework, collect responses as exit tickets, or run all 12 as a focused test prep review. Every problem includes a fully worked answer.

Quick reference: On a d–t graph, speed = slope = Δd ÷ Δt.  |  On a v–t graph, acceleration = slope = Δv ÷ Δt, and distance = area under the line.

Motion Graph Practice Problems

Problems progress from reading values directly off described graphs to interpreting slopes and areas, and on to full narrative descriptions of real-world journeys.

Easy — Reading Values Directly from Graphs

Easy Distance–Time Graph

Problem 1. The graph below shows a straight line passing through the origin and reaching a distance of 60 m at t = 10 s. What is the speed of the object?

Graph Description — Distance–Time Straight line from (0 s, 0 m) to (10 s, 60 m). The line is diagonal and continuous — no change in gradient.
Time (s)0246810
Distance (m)01224364860

Answer: Speed = slope = Δd ÷ Δt = 60 ÷ 10 = 6 m/s

Easy Distance–Time Graph

Problem 2. A graph shows a horizontal straight line at d = 40 m from t = 0 s to t = 8 s. What does this tell you about the object's motion?

Graph Description — Distance–Time Flat horizontal line at distance = 40 m across the full time range 0–8 s. No rise or fall.

Answer: A horizontal line on a d–t graph means the distance is not changing — the object is stationary (at rest). Its speed is 0 m/s.

Easy Velocity–Time Graph

Problem 3. A velocity–time graph shows a straight line from (0 s, 0 m/s) rising to (8 s, 16 m/s). What is the object's acceleration?

Graph Description — Velocity–Time Straight diagonal line from the origin (0, 0) rising uniformly to (8 s, 16 m/s).
Time (s)02468
Velocity (m/s)0481216

Answer: Acceleration = slope = Δv ÷ Δt = 16 ÷ 8 = 2 m/s²

Easy Velocity–Time Graph

Problem 4. A velocity–time graph shows a horizontal line at v = 12 m/s from t = 0 s to t = 10 s. What does this tell you about the object? What distance does it cover?

Graph Description — Velocity–Time Flat horizontal line at velocity = 12 m/s from t = 0 to t = 10 s.

Answer: A horizontal line on a v–t graph means constant velocity — zero acceleration. Distance = area under graph = 12 × 10 = 120 m

Medium — Interpreting Slope, Speed, and Area

Medium Distance–Time Graph

Problem 5. A distance–time graph has two straight-line segments. Segment A runs from (0 s, 0 m) to (5 s, 20 m). Segment B runs from (5 s, 20 m) to (10 s, 50 m). Which segment represents greater speed, and what are the two speeds?

Graph Description — Distance–Time (two segments) Segment A: shallower slope from (0, 0) to (5 s, 20 m). Segment B: steeper slope continuing from (5 s, 20 m) to (10 s, 50 m).

Answer: Speed A = 20 ÷ 5 = 4 m/s  |  Speed B = 30 ÷ 5 = 6 m/s  →  Segment B is faster (6 m/s vs 4 m/s) — the steeper slope always means greater speed on a d–t graph.

Medium Velocity–Time Graph

Problem 6. A velocity–time graph shows a straight line starting at v = 10 m/s at t = 0 s and falling to v = 0 m/s at t = 5 s. Calculate the deceleration and the total distance travelled.

Graph Description — Velocity–Time (deceleration) Straight line sloping downward from (0 s, 10 m/s) to (5 s, 0 m/s). Forms a right triangle with the time axis.

Answer: Deceleration = Δv ÷ Δt = (0 − 10) ÷ 5 = −2 m/s²  |  Distance = area of triangle = ½ × base × height = ½ × 5 × 10 = 25 m

Medium Distance–Time Graph

Problem 7. Two cyclists, A and B, appear on the same distance–time graph. Both start from the same point at t = 0. After 8 seconds, Cyclist A has covered 48 m and Cyclist B has covered 32 m. What are their speeds? At t = 8 s, how far apart are they?

Graph Description — Two Lines on Same D–T Graph Both lines start at (0, 0). Cyclist A's line is steeper, reaching (8 s, 48 m). Cyclist B's line is shallower, reaching (8 s, 32 m).

Answer: SpeedA = 48 ÷ 8 = 6 m/s  |  SpeedB = 32 ÷ 8 = 4 m/s  |  Gap = 48 − 32 = 16 m apart

Medium Velocity–Time Graph

Problem 8. A velocity–time graph shows three phases: Phase 1 — a straight line rising from (0 s, 0 m/s) to (4 s, 20 m/s). Phase 2 — a horizontal line at 20 m/s from (4 s) to (10 s). Phase 3 — a straight line falling from (10 s, 20 m/s) to (14 s, 0 m/s). Calculate the total distance travelled.

Graph Description — Velocity–Time (three phases) Triangle rising to 20 m/s over 4 s, then flat at 20 m/s for 6 s, then falling back to 0 over 4 s.

Answer: Phase 1 (triangle): ½ × 4 × 20 = 40 m  |  Phase 2 (rectangle): 6 × 20 = 120 m  |  Phase 3 (triangle): ½ × 4 × 20 = 40 m  →  Total = 40 + 120 + 40 = 200 m

Application — Describing and Interpreting Real-World Motion

Application Distance–Time Graph

Problem 9. A distance–time graph describes a dog's walk. Segment 1: straight line from (0 s, 0 m) to (10 s, 30 m). Segment 2: horizontal line from (10 s, 30 m) to (20 s, 30 m). Segment 3: straight line from (20 s, 30 m) to (30 s, 0 m). Describe the dog's motion in plain English for each phase, including speeds.

Graph Description — Dog's Journey (three segments) Rising line, then flat, then falling back to zero distance (return journey).

Answer: Segment 1: The dog walks away at 3 m/s (30 ÷ 10) for 10 seconds.  |  Segment 2: The dog stops and rests for 10 seconds (speed = 0).  |  Segment 3: The dog returns to the start at 3 m/s (30 ÷ 10) — note the line falls back to zero, meaning it returned to its original position.

Application Distance–Time Graph

Problem 10. A distance–time graph shows a cyclist's journey. Segment A: (0 s, 0 m) to (6 s, 24 m) — straight line. Segment B: (6 s, 24 m) to (9 s, 24 m) — flat. Segment C: (9 s, 24 m) to (15 s, 60 m) — straight line. What is the cyclist's average speed for the entire 15-second journey? How does it compare to the speed in Segment C alone?

Graph Description — Cyclist's Journey Rising line to 24 m, pause, then steeper rising line reaching 60 m at 15 s.

Answer: Total distance = 60 m. Total time = 15 s. Average speed = 60 ÷ 15 = 4 m/s. Speed in Segment C = (60 − 24) ÷ (15 − 9) = 36 ÷ 6 = 6 m/s. The cyclist was faster in Segment C than the overall average — the rest period in Segment B pulled the average down.

Application Velocity–Time Graph

Problem 11. A velocity–time graph describes a car journey in four phases. Phase 1: rises from (0 s, 0 m/s) to (5 s, 15 m/s). Phase 2: flat at 15 m/s from (5 s) to (15 s). Phase 3: falls from (15 s, 15 m/s) to (20 s, 5 m/s). Phase 4: flat at 5 m/s from (20 s) to (25 s). Calculate the acceleration in Phase 1 and Phase 3, then find the total distance covered.

Graph Description — Car Journey (four phases) Rising line (0→15 m/s), flat at 15 m/s, declining line (15→5 m/s), flat at 5 m/s.

Answer: a1 = 15 ÷ 5 = 3 m/s²  |  a3 = (5 − 15) ÷ 5 = −2 m/s² (deceleration). Distances — Phase 1 (triangle): ½ × 5 × 15 = 37.5 m  |  Phase 2 (rectangle): 10 × 15 = 150 m  |  Phase 3 (trapezium): ½ × (15 + 5) × 5 = 50 m  |  Phase 4 (rectangle): 5 × 5 = 25 m  →  Total = 262.5 m

Application Both Graph Types

Problem 12. Object X has a distance–time graph showing a straight line from (0 s, 0 m) to (10 s, 80 m). Object Y has a velocity–time graph showing a horizontal line at v = 6 m/s for 10 seconds. (a) What is the speed of Object X? (b) How far does Object Y travel? (c) Which object travels further in 10 seconds, and by how much?

Graph Description — Object X (D–T): Straight line origin to (10 s, 80 m).  |  Object Y (V–T): Flat horizontal line at 6 m/s for 10 s.

Answer: (a) Speed of X = 80 ÷ 10 = 8 m/s. (b) Distance of Y = 6 × 10 = 60 m. (c) Object X travels further by 20 m (80 m vs. 60 m).

How to Use These Motion Graph Problems in Class

  • Bell Ringers: Display one Easy problem on the board. Sketch the described graph as students arrive and ask them to identify speed or motion type — a quick, visual warm-up that takes 3–4 minutes and gets students thinking before instruction starts.
  • Exit Tickets: Use one Application problem at the end of class. Asking students to describe motion in their own words reveals conceptual understanding — not just formula recall — before the next lesson.
  • Homework: Assign Problems 1–8 after covering both graph types. Problems 9–12 are strong extension tasks for students who need to practise writing full descriptions of motion and interpreting multi-phase journeys.
  • Review: Print the graph description boxes separately and have students sketch the graph themselves before answering. This doubles as a graph-drawing exercise — a common assessment skill at Grade 6–8 level.
  • Test Prep: Sort problems by graph type (d–t reading / d–t interpretation / v–t acceleration / v–t area) and run each group as a focused mini-quiz across the final week of the motion unit.
