Pressure Practice Problems (With Answers)

Pressure measures how much force is applied over a given area. The formula is p = F ÷ A, where p is pressure in Pascals (Pa), F is force in Newtons (N), and A is the contact area in square metres (m²). One Pascal equals one Newton per square metre — so the smaller the area a force acts on, the higher the pressure it creates. That's why a drawing pin punctures cork easily, while a flat thumb does not.

Pressure Practice Problems

Problems progress from direct formula calculations to multi-variable solving and real-world scenarios.

Easy — Direct p = F ÷ A Calculations

Easy

Problem 1. A force of 60 N acts on an area of 0.5 m². What is the pressure?

Answer: p = 60 ÷ 0.5 = 120 Pa

Easy

Problem 2. A box exerts a force of 200 N on the floor. The base of the box has an area of 0.4 m². What pressure does the box exert on the floor?

Answer: p = 200 ÷ 0.4 = 500 Pa

Easy

Problem 3. A 150 N force is applied to a surface with an area of 3 m². What is the pressure?

Answer: p = 150 ÷ 3 = 50 Pa

Easy

Problem 4. A 900 N force is applied uniformly over an area of 0.09 m². What is the pressure in Pascals?

Answer: p = 900 ÷ 0.09 = 10,000 Pa

Medium — Solve for Force or Area

Medium

Problem 5. A pressure of 250 Pa is exerted on a surface with an area of 0.8 m². What is the force being applied?

Answer: F = p × A = 250 × 0.8 = 200 N

Medium

Problem 6. A hydraulic piston exerts a force of 4,500 N and creates a pressure of 9,000 Pa. What is the area of the piston?

Answer: A = F ÷ p = 4,500 ÷ 9,000 = 0.5 m²

Medium

Problem 7. Two identical books, each exerting a force of 20 N, are placed on the same surface. Book A has a base area of 0.04 m². Book B has a base area of 0.02 m². Calculate the pressure each book exerts and find the difference.

Answer: pA = 20 ÷ 0.04 = 500 Pa  |  pB = 20 ÷ 0.02 = 1,000 Pa  →  Book B exerts 500 Pa more pressure.

Medium

Problem 8. A pressure of 1,200 Pa must not be exceeded on a fragile surface. If the maximum allowed force is 480 N, what is the minimum contact area required?

Answer: A = F ÷ p = 480 ÷ 1,200 = 0.4 m² (The object's base must be at least 0.4 m².)

Application — Real-World Word Problems

Application

Problem 9. A person with a weight of 600 N stands first in flat shoes (total sole area = 0.030 m²) and then on the tips of high heels (total contact area = 0.0003 m²). Calculate the pressure in each case and explain which is more damaging to a wooden floor.

Answer: pflat = 600 ÷ 0.030 = 20,000 Pa  |  pheels = 600 ÷ 0.0003 = 2,000,000 Pa. The heels exert 100× more pressure — far more likely to dent or damage the floor even though the person's weight is the same.

Application

Problem 10. A person (weight 700 N) walks across soft snow. With normal boots the foot area is 0.025 m². With snowshoes the area increases to 0.50 m². Calculate the pressure in both cases and explain why snowshoes prevent sinking.

Answer: pboots = 700 ÷ 0.025 = 28,000 Pa  |  psnowshoes = 700 ÷ 0.50 = 1,400 Pa. Snowshoes spread the same force over 20× more area, reducing pressure by 95% — so the snow can support the person's weight without compacting.

Application

Problem 11. A sharp kitchen knife has a blade edge area of 0.000002 m² (2 × 10⁻⁶ m²). A cook applies a downward force of 10 N. What pressure does the blade exert? How does this explain why sharp knives cut more easily than blunt ones?

Answer: p = 10 ÷ 0.000002 = 5,000,000 Pa (5 MPa). A blunt knife has a larger edge area, so the same force produces far less pressure — it crushes rather than cuts. A sharp blade concentrates force on a tiny area, generating extreme pressure that slices cleanly.

Application

Problem 12. A storage container (weight 12,000 N) is placed on four legs, each with a circular base. Each leg has a radius of 0.05 m. Calculate the total contact area of all four legs and the pressure exerted on the floor. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Answer: Area of one leg = π × r² = 3.14 × 0.0025 = 0.00785 m²  →  Total area = 4 × 0.00785 = 0.0314 m²  →  p = 12,000 ÷ 0.0314 ≈ 382,166 Pa ≈ 382 kPa

How to Use These Pressure Problems in Class

  • Bell Ringers: Display one Easy problem as students enter. A single p = F ÷ A calculation takes 3–4 minutes and immediately connects to everyday examples students already know — shoes, tyres, or drawing pins.
  • Exit Tickets: Choose one Application problem and have students show their working before leaving. Their responses quickly reveal whether they understand pressure as a ratio, not just a number.
  • Homework: Assign Problems 1–8 after introducing the formula. Problems 9–12 make excellent extension work for students who are ready to apply pressure concepts to real-life design and engineering scenarios.
  • Review: Print all 12 problems on half-sheets and set up a classroom circuit. Students work through one problem per station every 4–5 minutes — high energy, low prep, great for the day before a test.
  • Test Prep: Group problems by skill type (solve for p / solve for F / solve for A / word problems) and use each group as a targeted 5-minute mini-quiz across the final week of the unit.
