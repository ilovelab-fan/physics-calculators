Speed & Velocity Practice Problems (With Answers)

Speed and velocity are closely related but not the same thing. Speed tells us how fast an object is moving — calculated with v = s ÷ t (distance divided by time) — but it has no direction. Velocity includes direction as well, making it a vector quantity. A car travelling at 60 km/h has a speed; a car travelling at 60 km/h due north has a velocity. At the middle school level, most problems focus on the v = s/t formula and its rearrangements: s = v × t and t = s ÷ v.

Speed & Velocity Practice Problems

Problems progress from direct formula calculations to rearranged solving and real-world scenarios.

Easy — Direct v = s ÷ t Calculations

Easy

Problem 1. A cyclist travels 120 m in 20 seconds. What is the cyclist's speed?

Answer: v = 120 ÷ 20 = 6 m/s

Easy

Problem 2. A runner completes a 400 m track in 50 seconds. What is their average speed?

Answer: v = 400 ÷ 50 = 8 m/s

Easy

Problem 3. A car drives 180 km in 2 hours. What is its average speed in km/h?

Answer: v = 180 ÷ 2 = 90 km/h

Easy

Problem 4. A snail moves 0.5 m in 100 seconds. What is its speed in m/s?

Answer: v = 0.5 ÷ 100 = 0.005 m/s

Medium — Solve for Distance or Time

Medium

Problem 5. A train travels at a constant speed of 75 km/h. How far does it travel in 3.5 hours?

Answer: s = v × t = 75 × 3.5 = 262.5 km

Medium

Problem 6. A swimmer moves at 1.5 m/s. How long will it take them to swim 90 m?

Answer: t = s ÷ v = 90 ÷ 1.5 = 60 seconds

Medium

Problem 7. Two objects start from the same point at the same time. Object A moves at 12 m/s. Object B moves at 9 m/s. After 8 seconds, how far apart are they?

Answer: sA = 12 × 8 = 96 m  |  sB = 9 × 8 = 72 m  →  Distance apart = 96 − 72 = 24 m

Medium

Problem 8. A bus must cover 210 km to reach its destination on time in 3 hours. For the first 2 hours it travels at 60 km/h. What speed must it maintain for the final hour to arrive on time?

Answer: Distance covered = 60 × 2 = 120 km  →  Remaining = 210 − 120 = 90 km in 1 hour  →  Required speed = 90 km/h

Application — Real-World Word Problems

Application

Problem 9. A school is 2.4 km from a student's home. The student walks at an average speed of 1.2 m/s. How many minutes will the journey take? (Convert km to m first.)

Answer: Distance = 2,400 m  →  t = 2,400 ÷ 1.2 = 2,000 s  →  2,000 ÷ 60 ≈ 33.3 minutes

Application

Problem 10. A cheetah runs at 28 m/s and a gazelle runs at 22 m/s. The cheetah starts a chase from 180 m behind the gazelle. Both run in the same direction. How long does it take the cheetah to close the gap and reach the gazelle?

Answer: Closing speed = 28 − 22 = 6 m/s (cheetah gaining)  →  t = 180 ÷ 6 = 30 seconds

Application

Problem 11. A car and a truck set off from opposite ends of a 240 km motorway at the same time, heading toward each other. The car travels at 90 km/h and the truck at 70 km/h. After how many hours do they meet, and how far has each vehicle travelled?

Answer: Combined speed = 90 + 70 = 160 km/h  →  t = 240 ÷ 160 = 1.5 hours  →  Car: 90 × 1.5 = 135 km  |  Truck: 70 × 1.5 = 105 km

Application

Problem 12. A probe is launched toward Mars. At one point in its journey it is travelling at 24,000 m/s. The distance remaining to Mars is 54,000,000 km. Convert this distance to metres and calculate — at this constant speed — how many days would remain before arrival?

Answer: Distance = 54,000,000 × 1,000 = 5.4 × 10¹⁰ m  →  t = 5.4 × 10¹⁰ ÷ 24,000 = 2,250,000 s  →  2,250,000 ÷ 86,400 ≈ 26 days

How to Use These Speed & Velocity Problems in Class

  • Bell Ringers: Post one Easy problem as students arrive. A single v = s ÷ t calculation takes under 3 minutes and gives you a clean way to open any lesson on motion, graphs, or kinematics.
  • Exit Tickets: Use one Application problem at the end of class and have students show their full working. You'll immediately see who has mastered the formula triangle and who needs a reteach before the next lesson.
  • Homework: Assign Problems 1–8 after introducing the three formula rearrangements. Problems 9–12 make strong extension tasks — they involve unit conversion and multi-step reasoning that stretches higher-ability students.
  • Review: Print all 12 on half-sheets and run a rotation activity — one problem per station, 4 minutes each. The variety of contexts (animals, vehicles, space) keeps the energy high on review days.
  • Test Prep: Sort problems by solve-for type (speed / distance / time / word problem) and assign each category as a focused 5-minute mini-quiz across the days leading up to a unit assessment.
