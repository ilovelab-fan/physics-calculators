Newton's Second Law of Motion states that the net force acting on an object equals its mass multiplied by its acceleration: F = m × a. The greater the force applied, the greater the acceleration — and the greater the mass, the more force you need to achieve the same acceleration. It's the foundation of classical mechanics and one of the most tested concepts in middle school physical science.

Newton's Second Law Practice Problems

Problems progress from direct formula practice to multi-variable solving and real-world scenarios.

Easy — Direct F = ma Calculations

Easy

Problem 1. A 5 kg object is pushed with a net force of 20 N. What is its acceleration?

Answer: a = F ÷ m = 20 ÷ 5 = 4 m/s²

Easy

Problem 2. A skateboard and rider have a combined mass of 60 kg. A force of 120 N is applied forward. What is the acceleration?

Answer: a = 120 ÷ 60 = 2 m/s²

Easy

Problem 3. A 10 kg shopping cart accelerates at 3 m/s². What net force is acting on it?

Answer: F = m × a = 10 × 3 = 30 N

Easy

Problem 4. A net force of 50 N causes an object to accelerate at 10 m/s². What is the mass of the object?

Answer: m = F ÷ a = 50 ÷ 10 = 5 kg

Medium — Solve for Different Variables

Medium

Problem 5. A 1,200 kg car accelerates from rest to a velocity requiring a net force of 3,600 N. What is the car's acceleration? If that acceleration is maintained for 5 seconds, what velocity does it reach?

Answer: a = 3,600 ÷ 1,200 = 3 m/s²  →  v = a × t = 3 × 5 = 15 m/s

Medium

Problem 6. Two forces act on a 8 kg box sitting on a frictionless surface: 35 N to the right and 11 N to the left. What is the net force and the resulting acceleration?

Answer: Fnet = 35 − 11 = 24 N (right)  →  a = 24 ÷ 8 = 3 m/s² to the right

Medium

Problem 7. An object accelerates at 6 m/s² when a force of 48 N is applied. A second, identical object is given the same force but accelerates at only 4 m/s². What is the mass of the second object?

Answer: m = F ÷ a = 48 ÷ 4 = 12 kg (The first object has mass 8 kg; the second is heavier.)

Medium

Problem 8. A 25 kg child on a sled experiences a forward push of 100 N and a friction force of 25 N opposing motion. What is the child's acceleration?

Answer: Fnet = 100 − 25 = 75 N  →  a = 75 ÷ 25 = 3 m/s²

Application — Real-World Word Problems

Application

Problem 9. A spacecraft with a mass of 2,000 kg fires its engine, producing a thrust of 10,000 N. There is no air resistance in space. What is the spacecraft's acceleration? If thrust doubles but mass stays the same, what happens to acceleration?

Answer: a = 10,000 ÷ 2,000 = 5 m/s². If thrust doubles to 20,000 N: a = 20,000 ÷ 2,000 = 10 m/s² — acceleration also doubles.

Application

Problem 10. A soccer player kicks a 0.45 kg ball and it accelerates at 80 m/s². A second player kicks the same ball with twice the force. What is the new acceleration?

Answer: F1 = 0.45 × 80 = 36 N  →  F2 = 72 N  →  a2 = 72 ÷ 0.45 = 160 m/s²

Application

Problem 11. A 70 kg person stands on a scale inside an elevator. The elevator accelerates upward at 2 m/s². The scale measures the normal force. What does the scale read? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: Weight = 70 × 10 = 700 N  |  Extra force needed = 70 × 2 = 140 N  →  Scale reads 700 + 140 = 840 N

Application

Problem 12. A truck (mass 3,000 kg) and a bicycle (mass 15 kg) are both acted on by a net force of 300 N. Compare their accelerations and explain what this tells us about Newton's Second Law.

Answer: atruck = 300 ÷ 3,000 = 0.1 m/s²  |  abicycle = 300 ÷ 15 = 20 m/s². The bicycle accelerates 200× faster — the same force produces far greater acceleration on a smaller mass, confirming that acceleration is inversely proportional to mass.

How to Use These Newton's Laws Problems in Class

  • Bell Ringers: Display one Easy problem as students settle in. A single F = ma calculation takes under 3 minutes and primes students for the day's lesson on forces.
