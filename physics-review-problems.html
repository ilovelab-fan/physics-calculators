Mixed Physics Review Problems

Each problem is labelled with its topic and difficulty. No formula is given — students must identify and apply the correct one, just as on a real test.

Problems 1–4

Easy Density

Problem 1. A piece of metal has a mass of 390 g and a volume of 50 cm³. Calculate its density and identify whether it is likely to be aluminium (2.7 g/cm³), iron (7.87 g/cm³), or copper (8.96 g/cm³).

Answer: ρ = 390 ÷ 50 = 7.8 g/cm³  →  most likely iron (density of iron ≈ 7.87 g/cm³).

Easy Speed

Problem 2. A cyclist covers 3.6 km in 12 minutes. Calculate the average speed in m/s. (Convert both distance and time to SI units first.)

Answer: Distance = 3,600 m  |  Time = 720 s  →  v = 3,600 ÷ 720 = 5 m/s

Easy Forces

Problem 3. A 15 kg box sits on a flat surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: 80 N to the right and 50 N to the left. Calculate the net force and the resulting acceleration.

Answer: Fnet = 80 − 50 = 30 N (right)  →  a = F ÷ m = 30 ÷ 15 = 2 m/s² to the right

Easy Energy

Problem 4. A 3 kg ball is dropped from a height of 10 m. Calculate its gravitational potential energy at the top. What is its kinetic energy just before it hits the ground? (Use g = 10 m/s², assume no air resistance.)

Answer: PE = 3 × 10 × 10 = 300 J. By conservation of energy, KE at ground = 300 J (all PE has converted to KE).

Problems 5–8

Medium Pressure

Problem 5. A 60 kg student stands on one foot during a science demo. The area of the sole of one shoe is 0.018 m². Calculate the pressure exerted on the floor. (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: Weight = 60 × 10 = 600 N  →  p = 600 ÷ 0.018 ≈ 33,333 Pa (≈ 33.3 kPa)

Medium Acceleration

Problem 6. A motorbike accelerates from 8 m/s to 26 m/s in 6 seconds. Calculate the acceleration. If the engine then cuts out and the bike decelerates at 3 m/s², how long does it take to stop from 26 m/s?

Answer: a = (26 − 8) ÷ 6 = 3 m/s²  |  Stopping time = 26 ÷ 3 ≈ 8.67 seconds

Medium Work & Power

Problem 7. A student pushes a 200 N trolley 15 m across a gym in 20 seconds. Calculate the work done and the student's average power output.

Answer: W = 200 × 15 = 3,000 J  →  P = 3,000 ÷ 20 = 150 W

Medium Unit Conversion

Problem 8. A science problem gives: force = 2.5 kN, distance = 80 cm, time = 2 minutes. Convert all three values to standard SI units, then calculate the work done and the power output.

Answer: F = 2,500 N  |  d = 0.8 m  |  t = 120 s  →  W = 2,500 × 0.8 = 2,000 J  →  P = 2,000 ÷ 120 ≈ 16.7 W

Problems 9–12

Application Motion Graphs

Problem 9. A velocity–time graph shows: Phase 1 — a straight line rising from (0 s, 0 m/s) to (5 s, 20 m/s). Phase 2 — a horizontal line at 20 m/s from (5 s) to (13 s). Phase 3 — a straight line falling from (13 s, 20 m/s) to (17 s, 0 m/s). Calculate the total distance covered and the acceleration in Phase 1.

Answer: Phase 1 (triangle): ½ × 5 × 20 = 50 m  |  Phase 2 (rectangle): 8 × 20 = 160 m  |  Phase 3 (triangle): ½ × 4 × 20 = 40 m  →  Total = 250 m. Acceleration (Phase 1) = 20 ÷ 5 = 4 m/s²

Application Mixed — Density + Pressure

Problem 10. A solid aluminium cube has sides of 0.1 m. The density of aluminium is 2,700 kg/m³. (a) Calculate the volume of the cube. (b) Calculate the mass of the cube. (c) Calculate the pressure the cube exerts on the surface it rests on — the contact area is its base (0.1 m × 0.1 m). (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: (a) V = 0.1³ = 0.001 m³  |  (b) m = ρ × V = 2,700 × 0.001 = 2.7 kg  |  (c) Weight = 2.7 × 10 = 27 N  →  A = 0.01 m²  →  p = 27 ÷ 0.01 = 2,700 Pa

Application Mixed — Energy + Speed

Problem 11. A 2 kg toy car rolls down a ramp. At the top of the ramp (height = 0.5 m) it is at rest. At the bottom it has converted all its potential energy to kinetic energy. (a) Calculate the PE at the top. (b) Calculate the speed at the bottom. (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: (a) PE = 2 × 10 × 0.5 = 10 J  |  (b) KE = 10 J  →  ½mv² = 10  →  v² = (2 × 10) ÷ 2 = 10  →  v = √10 ≈ 3.16 m/s

Application Mixed — Force + Work + Power

Problem 12. A 900 kg car accelerates from rest to 20 m/s in 10 seconds against a constant friction force of 500 N. (a) Calculate the acceleration. (b) Calculate the net force. (c) Calculate the engine force. (d) Calculate the work done by the engine over a 200 m distance and the engine's average power output.

Answer: (a) a = 20 ÷ 10 = 2 m/s²  |  (b) Fnet = 900 × 2 = 1,800 N  |  (c) Engine force = Fnet + friction = 1,800 + 500 = 2,300 N  |  (d) W = 2,300 × 200 = 460,000 J (460 kJ)  →  P = 460,000 ÷ 10 = 46,000 W (46 kW)

How to Use These Review Problems in Class

  • Test Prep: Hand the full 12-problem set to students 3–5 days before a unit test. The mixed topics force students to choose their own formula — the exact skill assessed on real exams. Review answers the following day as a class.
  • Revision Warm-ups: During a revision week, post one problem per day as a daily warm-up. The topic badges mean you can select by theme while keeping the format varied and unpredictable.
  • Homework: Assign Problems 1–8 as a mid-unit check. Assign Problems 9–12 (multi-formula application problems) as pre-assessment homework in the final days before a test.
  • Quizzes: Select 4–6 problems by topic to create a targeted 10-minute quiz. Problems 10–12 in particular work well as short-answer assessments that test multi-step reasoning.
  • Stations: Print each problem on a half-sheet and place one per station around the room. Students rotate every 4–5 minutes. The formula reference box on this page can be printed as a desk card — or deliberately withheld to mimic exam conditions.
