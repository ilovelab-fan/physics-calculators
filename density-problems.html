Density Practice Problems (With Answers)

Free worksheet (12 problems with answers) — ready to use for Grades 6–8

Density tells us how much mass is packed into a given volume. It is calculated using the formula ρ = m ÷ V, where ρ is density, m is mass (in grams or kilograms), and V is volume (in cm³ or m³). A gold coin and a foam block can be the same size, but the gold is far denser — that's density in action.

Density Practice Problems

Problems progress from straightforward calculations to real-world word problems.

Easy — Direct Formula Practice

Easy

Problem 1. A rock has a mass of 50 g and a volume of 25 cm³. What is its density?

Answer: ρ = 50 ÷ 25 = 2 g/cm³

Easy

Problem 2. A block of wood has a mass of 120 g and a volume of 200 cm³. What is the density of the wood?

Answer: ρ = 120 ÷ 200 = 0.6 g/cm³

Easy

Problem 3. A liquid has a density of 1.2 g/cm³ and a volume of 50 cm³. What is its mass?

Answer: m = ρ × V = 1.2 × 50 = 60 g

Easy

Problem 4. A metal cube has a mass of 270 g and a density of 2.7 g/cm³. What is its volume?

Answer: V = m ÷ ρ = 270 ÷ 2.7 = 100 cm³

Medium — Multi-Step Calculations

Medium

Problem 5. A rectangular box measures 4 cm × 5 cm × 10 cm and has a mass of 600 g. What is the density of the material?

Answer: V = 4 × 5 × 10 = 200 cm³  →  ρ = 600 ÷ 200 = 3 g/cm³

Medium

Problem 6. A sample of iron has a density of 7.87 g/cm³. If the volume of the sample is 40 cm³, what is its mass? Round to the nearest gram.

Answer: m = 7.87 × 40 = 314.8 g ≈ 315 g

Medium

Problem 7. Two objects have the same volume of 80 cm³. Object A has a mass of 160 g. Object B has a mass of 240 g. Which object is denser, and by how much?

Answer: ρA = 2 g/cm³  |  ρB = 3 g/cm³  →  Object B is denser by 1 g/cm³

Medium

Problem 8. A cylinder has a radius of 3 cm and a height of 7 cm. Its mass is 1,188 g. What is the density of the material? (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Answer: V = π × r² × h = 3.14 × 9 × 7 = 197.82 cm³  →  ρ = 1,188 ÷ 197.82 ≈ 6.0 g/cm³

Application — Real-World Word Problems

Application

Problem 9. A student drops a plastic toy into a tank of water. The toy floats. The toy has a mass of 45 g and a volume of 60 cm³. The density of water is 1.0 g/cm³. Explain why the toy floats using density values.

Answer: ρtoy = 45 ÷ 60 = 0.75 g/cm³. Since 0.75 g/cm³ < 1.0 g/cm³, the toy is less dense than water, so it floats.

Application

Problem 10. A geologist finds an unknown mineral. Its mass is 180 g and its volume (measured by water displacement) is 20 cm³. The density of quartz is 2.65 g/cm³ and feldspar is 2.56 g/cm³. Is this mineral likely quartz, feldspar, or neither?

Answer: ρ = 180 ÷ 20 = 9 g/cm³. Neither — the density is much higher than both minerals. (It is closer to copper at 8.96 g/cm³.)

Application

Problem 11. A shipping crate measures 2 m × 1.5 m × 1 m and contains packaged goods with a total mass of 900 kg. The maximum density that a freight carrier will accept without a surcharge is 350 kg/m³. Will the shipper charge a surcharge?

Answer: V = 2 × 1.5 × 1 = 3 m³  →  ρ = 900 ÷ 3 = 300 kg/m³. No surcharge — 300 kg/m³ is below the 350 kg/m³ limit.

Application

Problem 12. Ocean water has a density of about 1.025 g/cm³. Fresh water has a density of 1.000 g/cm³. A swimmer has a body density of 0.98 g/cm³. Will the swimmer float more easily in the ocean or in a freshwater lake? Explain.

Answer: The swimmer floats in both (their density is less than either water), but they float more easily in the ocean because the greater density difference means more buoyant force acts on them.

How to Use These Density Problems in Class

  • Bell Ringers: Post one Easy or Medium problem on the board as students enter. Takes 3–5 minutes and activates prior knowledge before a lesson.
  • Exit Tickets: Assign a single Application problem at the end of class. Collect student responses to quickly assess who has mastered the formula and who needs more support.
  • Homework: Assign Problems 1–8 for independent practice after initial instruction, and Problems 9–12 as a challenge extension.
  • Review: Use the full set of 12 as a station rotation or gallery walk — print each problem on a half-sheet and post around the room.
  • Test Prep: Group problems by type (solve for density / mass / volume / real-world) and use each group as a targeted mini-quiz the week before a unit test.
