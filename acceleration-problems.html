Acceleration Practice Problems (With Answers)

Free worksheet (12 problems with answers) — ready to use for Grades 6–8

Acceleration is the rate at which an object's velocity changes over time. The formula is a = Δv ÷ t, where a is acceleration in m/s², Δv is the change in velocity (final velocity minus initial velocity) in m/s, and t is the time taken in seconds. Acceleration doesn't only mean speeding up — an object decelerates (negative acceleration) when it slows down, and it also accelerates whenever it changes direction, even at constant speed. Understanding this distinction is one of the key conceptual leaps in middle school physics.

Acceleration Practice Problems

Problems progress from direct formula calculations to solving for velocity or time, and on to real-world scenarios involving cars, braking, and motion.

Easy — Direct a = Δv ÷ t Calculations

Easy

Problem 1. A car increases its velocity from 0 m/s to 20 m/s in 5 seconds. What is its acceleration?

Answer: a = (20 − 0) ÷ 5 = 4 m/s²

Easy

Problem 2. A cyclist speeds up from 4 m/s to 10 m/s over 3 seconds. What is the acceleration?

Answer: a = (10 − 4) ÷ 3 = 6 ÷ 3 = 2 m/s²

Easy

Problem 3. A ball rolling down a ramp goes from rest to 6 m/s in 4 seconds. What is its acceleration?

Answer: a = (6 − 0) ÷ 4 = 1.5 m/s²

Easy

Problem 4. A skateboard slows from 8 m/s to 2 m/s in 6 seconds. What is the acceleration? Is it positive or negative?

Answer: a = (2 − 8) ÷ 6 = −6 ÷ 6 = −1 m/s² (negative — the skateboard is decelerating).

Medium — Solve for Velocity or Time

Medium

Problem 5. A rocket accelerates at 15 m/s² for 8 seconds, starting from rest. What is its final velocity?

Answer: Δv = a × t = 15 × 8 = 120 m/s  →  vf = 0 + 120 = 120 m/s

Medium

Problem 6. A car accelerates at 3 m/s² from an initial velocity of 10 m/s. What is its velocity after 7 seconds?

Answer: Δv = 3 × 7 = 21 m/s  →  vf = 10 + 21 = 31 m/s

Medium

Problem 7. A train travelling at 45 m/s applies its brakes and decelerates at 5 m/s². How long does it take to come to a complete stop?

Answer: t = Δv ÷ a = (45 − 0) ÷ 5 = 9 seconds

Medium

Problem 8. Object A accelerates at 4 m/s² from rest. Object B accelerates at 2.5 m/s² from an initial velocity of 6 m/s. After 10 seconds, which object has the greater velocity, and by how much?

Answer: vA = 0 + (4 × 10) = 40 m/s  |  vB = 6 + (2.5 × 10) = 31 m/s  →  Object A is faster by 9 m/s.

Application — Real-World Word Problems

Application

Problem 9. A family car goes from 0 to 100 km/h in 8 seconds. Convert 100 km/h to m/s, then calculate the car's acceleration in m/s². (Hint: divide km/h by 3.6 to convert to m/s.)

Answer: 100 ÷ 3.6 ≈ 27.78 m/s  →  a = (27.78 − 0) ÷ 8 ≈ 3.47 m/s²

Application

Problem 10. A driver is travelling at 30 m/s when they spot a hazard and brake. The car decelerates at 6 m/s². How long does it take to stop, and is this better or worse than a car that decelerates at 9 m/s²?

Answer: t = 30 ÷ 6 = 5 seconds to stop. At 9 m/s²: t = 30 ÷ 9 ≈ 3.3 seconds  →  Higher deceleration (9 m/s²) stops the car faster — better brakes create greater deceleration and shorter stopping time.

Application

Problem 11. A sprinter starts from rest and reaches 9 m/s in 3 seconds. They hold that speed for 5 seconds, then slow to a stop in 2 seconds. Calculate the acceleration for each phase of the run and describe the motion in each phase.

Answer: Phase 1 (0–3 s): a = 9 ÷ 3 = 3 m/s² (speeding up)  |  Phase 2 (3–8 s): a = 0 m/s² (constant velocity)  |  Phase 3 (8–10 s): a = (0 − 9) ÷ 2 = −4.5 m/s² (decelerating).

Application

Problem 12. A spacecraft in deep space fires its engine for 12 seconds, accelerating from 2,000 m/s to 2,600 m/s. It then fires again 20 seconds later for 8 seconds, reaching 2,900 m/s. Compare the acceleration in both engine burns. Which burn produced greater acceleration?

Answer: Burn 1: a = (2,600 − 2,000) ÷ 12 = 600 ÷ 12 = 50 m/s²  |  Burn 2: a = (2,900 − 2,600) ÷ 8 = 300 ÷ 8 = 37.5 m/s²  →  Burn 1 produced greater acceleration.

How to Use These Acceleration Problems in Class

  • Bell Ringers: Post one Easy problem as students settle in. A quick a = Δv ÷ t calculation takes 3–4 minutes and is a natural opener for any lesson on motion, forces, or Newton's Second Law.
  • Exit Tickets: Use one Application problem at the end of class. Seeing whether students correctly identify deceleration as negative acceleration tells you exactly where conceptual gaps remain before the next lesson.
  • Homework: Assign Problems 1–8 after direct instruction on the formula and its rearrangements. Problems 9–12 work well as an enrichment set — they involve unit conversion, multi-phase motion, and comparative analysis.
  • Review: Print all 12 on half-sheets and run a station rotation — one problem per station, 4–5 minutes each. The real-world contexts (sprinting, cars, spacecraft) keep energy high during revision lessons.
  • Test Prep: Group problems by variable (solve for a / solve for v / solve for t / word problems) and assign each group as a targeted mini-quiz across the final days before a unit assessment.
