Getting units right isn't just a formality — it's the difference between a correct answer and a completely wrong one. In physics, measurements only make sense when they are expressed in the right units, and converting between them accurately is a foundational skill for every topic from speed and density to force and energy. The metric system (SI units) organises measurements around base-10 relationships, which means most conversions involve multiplying or dividing by powers of 10 — once students see the pattern, it becomes fast and reliable.

Unit Conversion Practice Problems

Problems progress from single-step metric conversions to multi-step and real-world problems where incorrect units would give a wrong answer.

Easy — Single-Step Metric Conversions

Easy

Problem 1. Convert 3,500 cm to metres.

Answer: 3,500 ÷ 100 = 35 m (Divide by 100 because there are 100 cm in 1 m.)

Easy

Problem 2. Convert 4.75 kg to grams.

Answer: 4.75 × 1,000 = 4,750 g (Multiply by 1,000 because there are 1,000 g in 1 kg.)

Easy

Problem 3. Convert 8.2 km to metres.

Answer: 8.2 × 1,000 = 8,200 m (Multiply by 1,000 because there are 1,000 m in 1 km.)

Easy

Problem 4. Convert 450 mm to centimetres and then to metres.

Answer: 450 mm ÷ 10 = 45 cm  →  45 ÷ 100 = 0.45 m (Step 1: 10 mm = 1 cm. Step 2: 100 cm = 1 m.)

Medium — Multi-Step and Mixed Unit Conversions

Medium

Problem 5. A car is travelling at 72 km/h. Convert this speed to metres per second (m/s).

Answer: Method 1 — divide by 3.6: 72 ÷ 3.6 = 20 m/s Method 2 — convert km to m (×1,000) and hours to seconds (÷3,600): 72,000 ÷ 3,600 = 20 m/s. ✓

Medium

Problem 6. A science experiment takes 2 hours and 45 minutes. Express the total time in seconds.

Answer: 2 hr = 2 × 3,600 = 7,200 s  |  45 min = 45 × 60 = 2,700 s  →  Total = 7,200 + 2,700 = 9,900 s

Medium

Problem 7. A metal rod measures 2.35 m. Express this length in (a) centimetres, (b) millimetres, and (c) kilometres.

Answer: (a) 2.35 × 100 = 235 cm  |  (b) 2.35 × 1,000 = 2,350 mm  |  (c) 2.35 ÷ 1,000 = 0.00235 km

Medium

Problem 8. A bag of rice weighs 2.4 kg. A recipe requires 350 g of rice. How many grams of rice remain after the recipe? Express the remainder in both grams and kilograms.

Answer: 2.4 kg = 2,400 g  →  2,400 − 350 = 2,050 g  →  2,050 ÷ 1,000 = 2.05 kg

Application — Units in Real-World Physics Problems

Application

Problem 9. A student calculates the speed of a toy car as follows: distance = 150 cm, time = 3 s. She writes the answer as 50 cm/s. Her teacher asks her to express the speed in m/s and then in km/h. Convert both.

Answer: Speed = 50 cm/s  →  50 ÷ 100 = 0.5 m/s  →  0.5 × 3.6 = 1.8 km/h (To convert m/s to km/h, multiply by 3.6.)

Application

Problem 10. A student measures the density of a liquid as 0.85 g/cm³. Their teacher asks for the answer in kg/m³. Convert the density. (Note: 1 g/cm³ = 1,000 kg/m³)

Answer: 0.85 g/cm³ × 1,000 = 850 kg/m³ (This conversion matters when using SI units in physics formulas — using g/cm³ in an SI equation would give a wrong answer by a factor of 1,000.)

Application

Problem 11. A runner completes a 5 km race in 23 minutes and 20 seconds. (a) Convert the total time to seconds. (b) Convert the distance to metres. (c) Calculate the runner's average speed in m/s, rounded to 2 decimal places.

Answer: (a) 23 min = 1,380 s  +  20 s = 1,400 s  |  (b) 5 km = 5,000 m  |  (c) v = 5,000 ÷ 1,400 ≈ 3.57 m/s

Application

Problem 12. A physics problem gives these values: force = 0.5 kN, mass = 250 g, distance = 80 cm, time = 0.4 minutes. Convert every value to its standard SI unit (N, kg, m, s) before any calculation is attempted.

Answer: Force: 0.5 kN × 1,000 = 500 N  |  Mass: 250 g ÷ 1,000 = 0.25 kg  |  Distance: 80 cm ÷ 100 = 0.8 m  |  Time: 0.4 × 60 = 24 s (All values are now in SI base units and safe to substitute into any standard physics formula.)

How to Use These Unit Conversion Problems in Class

  • Bell Ringers: Post one Easy problem as students arrive — a quick single-step conversion takes 2–3 minutes and makes an excellent low-stakes opener for any science lesson, not just physics units.
  • Exit Tickets: Use Problem 12 (the full SI conversion set) as an end-of-unit check. Students who can correctly convert all four values are ready to work with physics formulas independently.
  • Homework: Assign Problems 1–8 after introducing the metric system. Problems 9–12 work well as extension tasks — they require students to apply conversions inside real physics contexts rather than in isolation.
  • Review: Print the quick reference box separately as a desk card and have students work through all 12 problems without it first, then self-check using the reference. This builds genuine recall rather than dependence on the table.
  • Test Prep: Unit conversion errors are one of the most common sources of lost marks on physics assessments. Assign the Application problems as a focused mini-quiz in the week before any unit test that involves formulas.
