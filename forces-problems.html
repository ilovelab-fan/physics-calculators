Forces Practice Problems (With Answers)

Free worksheet (12 problems with answers) — ready to use for Grades 6–8

A force is a push or pull that acts on an object. Forces come in many types — gravity pulls objects toward the Earth, friction opposes motion between surfaces, the normal force pushes back on an object resting on a surface, and applied forces are direct pushes or pulls from people or machines. Every force has both a size (measured in Newtons, N) and a direction, which is why two forces acting on the same object must be added or subtracted depending on whether they act in the same or opposite directions. The net force is the overall result of all forces combined: Fnet = ΣF. When the net force is zero, forces are balanced and the object stays at rest or moves at constant velocity. When the net force is non-zero, forces are unbalanced and the object accelerates.

These forces practice problems are built for middle school teachers who need dependable, curriculum-aligned questions that cover everything from identifying force types to calculating net force in multi-force scenarios. Use one as a bell ringer, assign a set for homework, collect responses as exit tickets, or run all 12 as a test prep review. Key rules: Forces in the same direction → add them.  |  Forces in opposite directions → subtract (smaller from larger).  |  Net force = 0 N → balanced (no acceleration).  |  Net force ≠ 0 N → unbalanced (object accelerates).

Forces Practice Problems

Problems progress from identifying force types and simple net force calculations to multi-force real-world scenarios involving friction, air resistance, and equilibrium.

Easy — Identifying Forces and Simple Net Force

Easy

Problem 1. A book sits motionless on a table. Name the two forces acting on it and state whether they are balanced or unbalanced.

Force Diagram ↑ Normal force (table pushing up on book) ↓ Weight / gravity (Earth pulling book down)

Answer: The two forces are gravity (weight) acting downward and the normal force acting upward. Since the book is not moving, these forces are balanced — the net force is 0 N.

Easy

Problem 2. A single horizontal force of 45 N pushes a box to the right on a frictionless surface. What is the net force on the box, and in which direction will it move?

Force Diagram → Applied force: 45 N (right) (No friction, no opposing force)

Answer: Fnet = 45 N to the right. The forces are unbalanced — the box will accelerate to the right.

Easy

Problem 3. Two people push a crate from opposite sides. Person A pushes right with 60 N. Person B pushes left with 60 N. Calculate the net force. Are the forces balanced or unbalanced?

Force Diagram ← Person B: 60 N (left)  [CRATE]  Person A: 60 N (right) →

Answer: Fnet = 60 − 60 = 0 N. The forces are balanced. The crate will not move (assuming it was already at rest).

Easy

Problem 4. A 5 kg object hangs from a rope and is stationary. What is the tension in the rope? What type of force is pulling the object downward? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Force Diagram ↑ Tension (rope pulling up) ↓ Weight = mg (gravity pulling down)

Answer: Weight = 5 × 10 = 50 N downward. Since the object is stationary (balanced), tension = 50 N upward. The downward force is gravity (weight).

Medium — Calculating Net Force with Direction

Medium

Problem 5. Three horizontal forces act on a trolley: 55 N to the right, 20 N to the left, and 15 N to the left. Calculate the net force and state its direction. Will the trolley accelerate?

Force Diagram ← 20 N   ← 15 N  [TROLLEY]  55 N →

Answer: Forces right = 55 N. Forces left = 20 + 15 = 35 N. Fnet = 55 − 35 = 20 N to the right. Forces are unbalanced — the trolley will accelerate to the right.

Medium

Problem 6. A box is pushed to the right with an applied force of 90 N. Friction acts to the left with 35 N. Calculate the net force. If the box has a mass of 10 kg, what is its acceleration?

Force Diagram ← Friction: 35 N  [BOX]  Applied: 90 N →

Answer: Fnet = 90 − 35 = 55 N to the right. Acceleration = F ÷ m = 55 ÷ 10 = 5.5 m/s²

Medium

Problem 7. A skydiver of weight 750 N is falling. At one moment, air resistance acting upward is 300 N. Calculate the net force and state the direction of acceleration. Is the skydiver speeding up or slowing down relative to terminal velocity?

Force Diagram ↑ Air resistance: 300 N ↓ Weight (gravity): 750 N

Answer: Fnet = 750 − 300 = 450 N downward. Forces are unbalanced — the skydiver is still accelerating downward (speeding up). Terminal velocity is not yet reached — that occurs when air resistance = weight = 750 N.

Medium

Problem 8. In a tug-of-war, Team A pulls left with a combined force of 480 N. Team B pulls right with 560 N. Calculate the net force, identify which team wins, and state whether the rope accelerates.

Force Diagram ← Team A: 480 N  [ROPE]  Team B: 560 N →

Answer: Fnet = 560 − 480 = 80 N to the right. Team B wins — the net force acts in their direction. The rope (and both teams) will accelerate to the right.

Application — Real-World Force Scenarios

Application

Problem 9. A car's engine produces a forward driving force of 4,000 N. Air resistance acts backward with 900 N, and rolling friction acts backward with 300 N. Calculate the net force and the car's acceleration if its mass is 1,200 kg. Is the car speeding up or travelling at constant speed?

Force Diagram ← Air resistance: 900 N ← Rolling friction: 300 N  [CAR]  → Engine force: 4,000 N

Answer: Opposing forces = 900 + 300 = 1,200 N. Fnet = 4,000 − 1,200 = 2,800 N forward. a = 2,800 ÷ 1,200 ≈ 2.33 m/s² — the car is speeding up.

Application

Problem 10. A picture frame hangs on a wall, held by two strings. String 1 pulls with 30 N upward-left and String 2 pulls with 30 N upward-right. The vertical components of both strings together support the frame. If the frame is in equilibrium, what must the weight of the frame be? What does this tell us about balanced forces?

Force Diagram ↑ String 1 (30 N) + String 2 (30 N) → combined vertical support = 60 N upward ↓ Weight = ?

Answer: For equilibrium, net force = 0. Total upward force = 30 + 30 = 60 N. Therefore weight = 60 N downward. This confirms that when forces are balanced, the object remains stationary — no net force means no acceleration.

Application

Problem 11. A boat moves at a constant velocity of 8 m/s across a lake. The engine produces a forward thrust of 1,500 N. What must the total resistive force (water resistance + drag) be? Explain your reasoning using Newton's First Law.

Force Diagram ← Resistive forces: ? N  [BOAT]  Engine thrust: 1,500 N →

Answer: Constant velocity means zero acceleration, which means net force = 0 N. Therefore resistive force = 1,500 N backward. By Newton's First Law, an object at constant velocity has balanced forces — the engine force is exactly cancelled by resistance.

Application

Problem 12. A 70 kg person stands in an elevator. The elevator accelerates upward at 2 m/s². (a) Calculate the person's weight. (b) Calculate the net upward force needed to produce this acceleration. (c) What is the normal force (scale reading) the floor must exert? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Force Diagram ↑ Normal force (floor pushing up) = ? ↓ Weight = mg (gravity pulling down) Net force = ma (upward, since accelerating up)

Answer: (a) Weight = 70 × 10 = 700 N downward. (b) Fnet = ma = 70 × 2 = 140 N upward. (c) Normal force = Weight + Fnet = 700 + 140 = 840 N upward. The person feels heavier because the floor must push harder than their weight to accelerate them upward.

How to Use These Forces Problems in Class

  • Bell Ringers: Display one Easy problem and ask students to draw a quick force diagram as they settle in. Getting students to sketch arrows before calculating forces builds the visual habit that prevents direction errors on assessments.
  • Exit Tickets: Use Problem 11 (the constant velocity boat) as an end-of-class check. Students who correctly identify the resistive force using Newton's First Law have understood the conceptual core — not just the arithmetic.
  • Homework: Assign Problems 1–8 after covering net force and balanced/unbalanced forces. Problems 9–12 work best as extension tasks once students are confident with direction — they require multi-force analysis and Newton's Laws together.
  • Review: Print the force diagram boxes separately and have students complete them before calculating. This turns each problem into both a diagram exercise and a calculation exercise — doubling the practice value from the same question.
  • Test Prep: Sort problems by concept (identify forces / net force calculation / balanced forces / unbalanced and acceleration) and assign each group as a focused mini-quiz in the week before a unit assessment.
