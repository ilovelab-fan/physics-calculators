In physics, work is done when a force moves an object through a distance in the direction of that force. The formula is W = F × d, where W is work in Joules (J), F is the applied force in Newtons (N), and d is the distance moved in metres (m). Importantly, if a force is applied but the object doesn't move — or moves perpendicular to the force — no work is done in the physics sense. Power measures how quickly that work is done: P = W ÷ t, where P is power in Watts (W) and t is time in seconds. A powerful machine doesn't necessarily do more work — it just does the same work faster.

Work & Power Practice Problems

Problems progress from direct formula calculations to solving for individual variables and real-world scenarios involving lifting, machines, and engines.

Easy — Direct W = F × d Calculations

Easy

Problem 1. A person pushes a trolley with a force of 50 N over a distance of 6 m. How much work is done?

Answer: W = 50 × 6 = 300 J

Easy

Problem 2. A student lifts a 20 N textbook from the floor to a shelf 1.5 m above. How much work does the student do?

Answer: W = 20 × 1.5 = 30 J

Easy

Problem 3. A horse pulls a plough with a constant force of 800 N across a field 200 m long. What is the total work done by the horse?

Answer: W = 800 × 200 = 160,000 J (160 kJ)

Easy

Problem 4. A crane lifts a 5,000 N load to a height of 12 m. How much work does the crane do?

Answer: W = 5,000 × 12 = 60,000 J (60 kJ)

Medium — Solve for Force, Distance, or Power

Medium

Problem 5. A motor does 1,200 J of work in 8 seconds. What is the power output of the motor?

Answer: P = W ÷ t = 1,200 ÷ 8 = 150 W

Medium

Problem 6. A machine with a power output of 400 W runs for 30 seconds. How much work does it do?

Answer: W = P × t = 400 × 30 = 12,000 J (12 kJ)

Medium

Problem 7. A worker does 4,500 J of work by pushing a crate along a floor. The force applied is 300 N. What distance was the crate pushed?

Answer: d = W ÷ F = 4,500 ÷ 300 = 15 m

Medium

Problem 8. Two workers both move a 2,000 N pallet a distance of 10 m. Worker A completes the job in 20 seconds. Worker B takes 50 seconds. Calculate the work and power for each worker. Who is more powerful?

Answer: Work (both) = 2,000 × 10 = 20,000 J  →  PA = 20,000 ÷ 20 = 1,000 W  |  PB = 20,000 ÷ 50 = 400 W  →  Worker A is more powerful — same work done in less time.

Application — Real-World Word Problems

Application

Problem 9. A 60 kg student climbs a flight of stairs 4 m high in 6 seconds. Calculate the work done against gravity and the student's power output. (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: F = mg = 60 × 10 = 600 N  →  W = 600 × 4 = 2,400 J  →  P = 2,400 ÷ 6 = 400 W

Application

Problem 10. A car engine produces a driving force of 3,500 N and moves the car 500 m along a road. The journey takes 25 seconds. Calculate the work done by the engine and its power output. Express power in both Watts and kilowatts.

Answer: W = 3,500 × 500 = 1,750,000 J (1,750 kJ)  →  P = 1,750,000 ÷ 25 = 70,000 W = 70 kW

Application

Problem 11. A construction lift raises building materials weighing 8,000 N to a height of 30 m. The lift motor has a power output of 6,000 W. How long does the lift take to complete the job? If a more powerful 10,000 W motor were used, how much time would be saved?

Answer: W = 8,000 × 30 = 240,000 J  →  t = W ÷ P = 240,000 ÷ 6,000 = 40 seconds. With 10,000 W motor: t = 240,000 ÷ 10,000 = 24 s  →  16 seconds saved.

Application

Problem 12. An electric pump lifts 500 litres of water per minute from a well 8 m deep. One litre of water weighs 10 N. Calculate the work done per minute and the minimum power the pump must have. (Express your answer in Watts.)

Answer: Total weight = 500 × 10 = 5,000 N  →  W = 5,000 × 8 = 40,000 J per minute  →  t = 60 s  →  P = 40,000 ÷ 60 ≈ 667 W

How to Use These Work & Power Problems in Class

  • Bell Ringers: Post one Easy problem as students settle in. A single W = F × d calculation takes 3–4 minutes and connects directly to concepts like energy, forces, and simple machines that students encounter across the unit.
  • Exit Tickets: Use one Application problem at the end of class and ask students to show all working. Their responses reveal whether they understand why work requires both force and displacement — a common source of confusion.
  • Homework: Assign Problems 1–8 after direct instruction on both formulas. Problems 9–12 make excellent extension tasks — they involve multi-step reasoning, unit conversion, and real engineering contexts.
  • Review: Print all 12 on half-sheets and set up a classroom circuit. Students rotate every 4–5 minutes — one problem per station. The real-world scenarios (stairs, cars, pumps, cranes) keep engagement high on review days.
  • Test Prep: Sort problems by type (solve for W / solve for P / solve for F or d / word problems) and assign each group as a focused 5-minute mini-quiz in the days leading up to a unit test.
