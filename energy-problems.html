Kinetic & Potential Energy Practice Problems (With Answers)

Energy comes in two fundamental mechanical forms. Kinetic energy (KE) is the energy of motion, calculated with KE = ½ × m × v² — the faster an object moves or the greater its mass, the more kinetic energy it carries. Potential energy (PE) is stored energy based on position, calculated with PE = m × g × h, where g = 9.8 m/s² (often rounded to 10 m/s² in class) and h is height above the ground. Together, these two formulas are the backbone of any middle school energy unit.

Kinetic & Potential Energy Practice Problems

Problems progress from direct formula practice to multi-variable solving and real-world scenarios.

Easy — Direct Formula Practice

Easy

Problem 1. A 4 kg ball is moving at 3 m/s. What is its kinetic energy?

Answer: KE = ½ × 4 × 3² = ½ × 4 × 9 = 18 J

Easy

Problem 2. A 2 kg book sits on a shelf 5 m above the floor. What is its gravitational potential energy? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: PE = m × g × h = 2 × 10 × 5 = 100 J

Easy

Problem 3. A 10 kg bicycle is moving at 6 m/s. What is its kinetic energy?

Answer: KE = ½ × 10 × 6² = ½ × 10 × 36 = 180 J

Easy

Problem 4. A 5 kg object is held 3 m above the ground. What is its gravitational potential energy? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: PE = 5 × 10 × 3 = 150 J

Medium — Solve for Different Variables

Medium

Problem 5. An object has a kinetic energy of 200 J and a mass of 8 kg. What is its speed?

Answer: KE = ½mv²  →  v² = (2 × KE) ÷ m = (2 × 200) ÷ 8 = 50  →  v = √50 ≈ 7.07 m/s

Medium

Problem 6. A rock has a gravitational potential energy of 490 J and is held 10 m above the ground. What is the mass of the rock? (Use g = 9.8 m/s²)

Answer: m = PE ÷ (g × h) = 490 ÷ (9.8 × 10) = 490 ÷ 98 = 5 kg

Medium

Problem 7. A 3 kg ball rolls down a hill. At the top it has PE = 360 J and KE = 0 J. At the bottom, all PE has converted to KE (ignore friction). What is the ball's speed at the bottom?

Answer: KEbottom = 360 J  →  v² = (2 × 360) ÷ 3 = 240  →  v = √240 ≈ 15.5 m/s

Medium

Problem 8. A 6 kg object moving at 4 m/s is lifted to a height of 8 m and brought to rest. How does its total mechanical energy change? Calculate both KE and PE. (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: KEinitial = ½ × 6 × 16 = 48 J  |  PEfinal = 6 × 10 × 8 = 480 J. Total energy increased — external work (432 J) was done on the object to lift and stop it.

Application — Real-World Word Problems

Application

Problem 9. A skateboarder (mass 50 kg) starts from rest at the top of a 4 m ramp. Assuming no friction, what is her speed at the bottom? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: PEtop = 50 × 10 × 4 = 2,000 J = KEbottom  →  v² = (2 × 2,000) ÷ 50 = 80  →  v = √80 ≈ 8.94 m/s

Application

Problem 10. A 0.15 kg baseball is pitched at 40 m/s. A heavier softball (mass 0.20 kg) is thrown at 30 m/s. Which ball has more kinetic energy, and by how much?

Answer: KEbaseball = ½ × 0.15 × 1,600 = 120 J  |  KEsoftball = ½ × 0.20 × 900 = 90 J  →  The baseball has 30 J more kinetic energy.

Application

Problem 11. A 1,000 kg roller coaster car is at rest at the top of a 25 m drop. At what height above the ground would its kinetic energy equal its remaining potential energy? (Use g = 10 m/s², start from h = 25 m)

Answer: Total PE = 1,000 × 10 × 25 = 250,000 J. KE = PE when each = 125,000 J  →  h = PE ÷ (m × g) = 125,000 ÷ 10,000 = 12.5 m above the ground (halfway down).

Application

Problem 12. A 2 kg pendulum bob is pulled 0.8 m above its lowest point and released. Assuming no air resistance, what is the maximum speed it reaches at the lowest point? What does this tell us about energy conservation? (Use g = 9.8 m/s²)

Answer: PEtop = 2 × 9.8 × 0.8 = 15.68 J = KEbottom  →  v² = (2 × 15.68) ÷ 2 = 15.68  →  v ≈ 3.96 m/s. This demonstrates the Law of Conservation of Energy: total mechanical energy stays constant — PE converts entirely to KE at the lowest point.

How to Use These Energy Problems in Class

  • Bell Ringers: Post one Easy problem on the board as students arrive. A single KE or PE calculation takes 3–4 minutes and connects directly to the day's lesson on energy transfer.
  • Exit Tickets: Use one Application problem as an end-of-class check. Collecting responses lets you identify which students can apply energy conservation before the next lesson.
  • Homework: Assign Problems 1–8 after direct instruction on both formulas. Problems 9–12 work well as an enrichment or extension set for students who are ready to go deeper.
  • Review: Print problems on half-sheets and set up a circuit — one problem per station. Students rotate every 4–5 minutes. Works especially well the day before a quiz or test.
  • Test Prep: Sort by formula type (KE / PE / conservation / word problems) and assign each type as a focused mini-quiz across the final week of the unit.
